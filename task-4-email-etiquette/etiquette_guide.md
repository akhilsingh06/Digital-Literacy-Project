# 10 Golden Rules of Email Etiquette for College Students

A practical guide to writing professional emails that make a strong impression — every time.

---

## ✉️ The 10 Golden Rules

---

### Rule 1 · Write a Clear, Specific Subject Line
> Your subject line is the first thing the recipient reads. Make it informative.

❌ **Poor:** `Hi` / `Question` / `Assignment`  
✅ **Good:** `Request for Extension – Digital Systems Assignment (CSE2001) | Akhil Singh`

A good subject line tells the reader *who you are*, *what the email is about*, and *why it matters* — before they even open it.

---

### Rule 2 · Use a Professional Greeting (Salutation)
Always begin with a formal address. Match the salutation to your relationship with the recipient.

| Recipient | Appropriate Greeting |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Professor / Faculty | `Dear Professor [Last Name],` |
| Unknown coordinator | `Dear Sir / Dear Ma'am,` or `To Whom It May Concern,` |
| Company recruiter | `Dear Mr./Ms. [Last Name],` |

Never use: `Hey`, `Yo`, `Hi there`, or just the person's first name in formal contexts.

---

### Rule 3 · Introduce Yourself in the First Line
The recipient receives dozens of emails daily. Always state who you are immediately.

✅ **Example:** *"I am Akhil Singh, a first-year B.Tech student in Computer Science at VIT Bhopal University (Reg. No. 24BCE10XXX)."*

---

### Rule 4 · State Your Purpose Clearly in the Opening Paragraph
Get to the point. Don't bury the reason for your email at the bottom.

✅ **Example:** *"I am writing to request a three-day extension on the Digital Systems assignment due on 5th April."*

---

### Rule 5 · Keep the Body Concise and Well-Structured
- Use short paragraphs (3–5 sentences max each).
- Break up content logically: introduction → context → request/information → closing.
- Avoid walls of text — if you have multiple points, use a numbered list.

---

### Rule 6 · Proofread Every Email Before Sending
Typos, grammatical errors, and incorrect names damage your credibility instantly. Always:
- Re-read the email once from the recipient's perspective.
- Use spell-check, but don't rely on it alone.
- Double-check the recipient's name and email address.

---

### Rule 7 · Use a Professional Sign-Off
End every formal email with a proper closing followed by your full name and details.

✅ **Appropriate closings:**
- `Yours sincerely,` (when you know the recipient's name)
- `Yours faithfully,` (when writing "Dear Sir/Ma'am")
- `With regards,` / `Best regards,`

❌ **Avoid:** `Cheers`, `Thanks!`, `Later`, or no sign-off at all.

---

### Rule 8 · Always Include Your Full Signature Block
Every formal email should include:
- Full name
- Registration / Student ID number
- Programme and year (e.g., B.Tech CSE, Year 1)
- Institution name
- Contact email (preferably your university email)

---

### Rule 9 · Use Your Institutional Email for All Formal Communication
Using `akhil.singh2024@vitbhopal.ac.in` is professional.  
Using `coolgamer007@gmail.com` is not — no matter how well written the email is.

Your institutional email immediately establishes your credibility and affiliation.

---

### Rule 10 · Respond Promptly — Within 24 Hours When Possible
If you receive an email from a professor, recruiter, or coordinator, acknowledge it quickly — even if you need more time to prepare a full response.

✅ **Quick acknowledgement:** *"Thank you for your email. I will review the feedback and respond in detail by [date]."*

Silence is often interpreted as disinterest or disrespect in professional communication.

---

## 📋 Quick-Reference Checklist

Before you hit **Send**, run through this checklist:

- [ ] Subject line is clear and specific
- [ ] Correct recipient name and email address
- [ ] Formal greeting used
- [ ] Introduced myself in the first line
- [ ] Purpose stated early and clearly
- [ ] Body is concise and logically structured
- [ ] No spelling or grammar errors (proofread twice)
- [ ] Professional sign-off included
- [ ] Full signature block at the bottom
- [ ] Sent from my institutional/professional email
